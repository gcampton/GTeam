# AI Writing Patterns — What to Avoid and How to Fix

Quick reference for reviewing AI-assisted content before publishing. Run through all 5 sections.

---

## 1. Hollow Opener Patterns (delete on sight)

These phrases appear in the first paragraph and signal AI generation to both readers and Google:

| Pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| "In today's fast-paced world..." | Cliché with zero information | Start with a specific claim or stat |
| "In the ever-evolving landscape of X..." | Same as above | State the specific change that matters |
| "It goes without saying that..." | If it goes without saying, don't say it | Delete the sentence or state it directly |
| "Whether you're a beginner or an expert..." | Tries to appeal to everyone, speaks to no one | Pick a specific audience |
| "In this article, we will explore..." | Restates the title | Start with the first real piece of value |
| "As we navigate [topic]..." | Vague and impersonal | "Navigating" is not a thought |
| "X is more important than ever..." | Why? When wasn't it important? | State the specific reason it matters now |
| "In conclusion, we have covered..." | Unnecessary summary sentence | Summarise the actual insight, not the structure |

**Rule:** The first sentence must do one of: make a claim, share a number, open a scenario, or challenge an assumption. If it doesn't, rewrite it.

---

## 2. Transition Words That Flag AI Generation

Heavy use of these in sequence is a pattern detector signal:

- Additionally / Furthermore / Moreover (use sparingly — max 1 per 500 words)
- It is worth noting that... (just note it)
- It is important to remember that... (just say what's important)
- In light of this... / With that in mind... / Having said that...
- Last but not least... (never use this)

**Fix:** Replace with direct connection or restructure into separate sentences. "X is true. Y follows from this." is cleaner than "Additionally, Y is also an important consideration."

---

## 3. Vague Generalities (replace with specifics)

| AI pattern | Human replacement |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Many businesses struggle with X" | "67% of [category] companies report X — [source]" |
| "There are several key factors to consider" | "Three factors determine the outcome: [A], [B], [C]" |
| "This can lead to significant improvements" | "This reduced churn by 23% in [case study]" |
| "Experts recommend..." | "[Name], [title] at [org], recommends..." |
| "Recent studies show..." | "[Study name, year] found that..." |
| "X can be challenging" | "X fails at [specific step] because [specific reason]" |
| "It's essential to..." | Delete "it's essential to" — state the action directly |
| "Various strategies exist for..." | List the strategies |

**Rule:** Every paragraph must contain at least one specific: a number, a name, a source, or a concrete example.

---

## 4. Structure Patterns That Signal AI

**Uniform paragraph length:** Every paragraph 3–4 sentences. Fix: vary between 1-sentence punches and 4–5 sentence explanations.

**Uniform sentence length:** Every sentence 20–25 words. Fix: mix short declarative sentences with longer explanatory ones.

**The "three-part list with equal weighting" pattern:** AI consistently divides topics into exactly 3 parts with identical structure. Fix: let the content determine the structure, not the other way around. Sometimes there are 2 things, sometimes 7.

**The padded conclusion:** "In summary, we have explored X, Y, and Z. These insights will help you [achieve vague goal]." Fix: End with a specific action, a single key takeaway, or a provocative question — not a recap.

**Symmetrical H2 sections:** Every H2 section the same length, same number of subpoints. Fix: let important sections be long; let simple sections be short.

## 5. E-E-A-T Signals That Must Be Present

Google's E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) favours content with these markers. AI-generated content typically lacks them unless deliberately added.

**Experience signals:**
- Specific real outcomes: "When we ran this test, conversion dropped 12% before we found the cause"
- Named specific tools, platforms, or situations: not "a CRM" but "HubSpot's contact timeline"
- Unexpected details that only someone with experience would know
- Acknowledgement of what doesn't work or common mistakes

**Expertise signals:**
- Author byline with credentials (not "Editorial Team")
- Named citations from credible primary sources (not "research shows")
- Acknowledged nuance: "This works in X context but fails in Y because..."
- Technical depth where appropriate — not surface-level treatment

**Authoritativeness signals:**
- Links to primary sources (government, academic, established industry bodies)
- Cited statistics with source and year
- Original data or research

**Trustworthiness signals:**
- Publish date visible
- Last-reviewed date for evergreen content
- Factual claims have citations
- Corrections policy for substantive errors

**Sections to flag `[NEEDS OWNER INPUT]` if none of the above are present:**
Any section making a claim about outcomes, efficacy, recommendations, or best practices without a firsthand example or primary source citation.

## 6. YMYL Extra Requirements

For health, finance, legal, or safety content — every single one of these must be met before publishing:

- [ ] Every factual claim has a primary source (not a secondary article)
- [ ] Disclaimer present: "This is not [medical/legal/financial] advice. Consult a qualified [professional] for your specific situation."
- [ ] Author has stated, relevant credentials (or "reviewed by [expert]" with their credentials)
- [ ] Content reviewed by a qualified human in the relevant field
- [ ] Last-reviewed date is current (within 12 months for fast-moving topics)
- [ ] No absolute claims ("X always works", "X is safe") — use qualified language ("evidence suggests", "in most cases")
