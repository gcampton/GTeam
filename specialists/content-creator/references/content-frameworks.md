# Content Writing Frameworks

> **Confidence:** All recommendations are `[HYPOTHESIS]` (untested best-practice) unless marked `[TESTED: date]` or `[REVISED: date]`. Check `../results/` for empirical outcomes before applying advice.

Use this as a practical reference when structuring copy. Pick the framework that matches reader intent and funnel stage — not the one you're most comfortable with.

---

## Writing Frameworks

### AIDA — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action

**When to use:** Landing pages, ads, cold outreach, product pages. Works best when the reader has moderate awareness of the problem.

`[HYPOTHESIS]` AIDA is the most durable framework for conversion-focused copy because each stage eliminates a specific objection before the next stage builds.

**Landing page skeleton:**

```
[HEADLINE — Attention]
One sentence that stops the scroll. Specific, not clever.
e.g., "You're losing 3 hours a week to inbox management."

[SUBHEADLINE / LEAD — Interest]
Expand the problem. Show you understand their world.
2–3 sentences, no solution yet.

[BODY — Desire]
Show the transformed state. Benefits over features.
Use social proof here: numbers, names, outcomes.
"2,400 teams reduced meeting time by 40% in their first month."

[CTA — Action]
Single action. Verb + outcome.
"Start your free 14-day trial" (button)
```

### PAS — Problem → Agitate → Solution

**When to use:** Sales email intros, social posts, webinar openers, any cold audience that doesn't yet feel the pain acutely.

`[HYPOTHESIS]` PAS outperforms AIDA for cold email opens because it meets the reader in their current emotional state before making any pitch.

**Sales email intro skeleton:**

```
[PROBLEM — 1 sentence]
Name the exact problem without editorialising.
"Most SaaS founders spend 60% of their time on tasks that don't compound."

[AGITATE — 2–3 sentences]
Make the problem feel real and costly. Specifics beat adjectives.
"That's 3 days every week that don't build your moat.
Every hour on Notion templates is an hour not talking to a customer.
After 6 months, that gap is impossible to close."

[SOLUTION — 1 paragraph]
Introduce the solution as relief, not a product pitch.
"We built [X] specifically to remove those 3 days from your calendar.
Here's how one team got them back:"
```

### Before / After / Bridge

**When to use:** Testimonials, case studies, product explainers, intro sections. Strong when you have real outcome data.

| Stage | What it does | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Before** | Name the painful current state | "Our sales team tracked every deal in a spreadsheet. We missed follow-ups constantly." |
| **After** | Describe the desirable future state | "Now every rep knows exactly what to do next. Nothing falls through." |
| **Bridge** | The product/idea that connects them | "[Product] gave us one view of every deal and automated the follow-up queue." |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Before/After/Bridge is the most effective testimonial structure because it mirrors how humans naturally tell stories about change.

### The Inverted Pyramid

**When to use:** News writing, web copy, email body text, any context where readers skim and may leave early.

```
[Most important information first]
Who, what, when, where, why — answer the core question immediately.

[Supporting detail]
Evidence, context, secondary facts.

[Background / nice-to-know]
History, tangential detail, deeper explanation.
```

`[HYPOTHESIS]` On web pages, 55% of readers spend fewer than 15 seconds on a page (Nielsen). Inverted pyramid ensures the most critical message is delivered before they leave.

**Practical rule:** Write your conclusion first, then ask "what do they need to know to believe this?" and add it below.

## Hook Types

Every piece starts with a hook. The hook earns the second sentence.

| Hook Type | Template | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Surprising statistic** | "[Number] that changes how you think about [topic]" | "83% of B2B deals are lost before a sales call happens." |
| **Contrarian claim** | "[Widely-held belief] is wrong — here's why" | "More content does not mean more traffic." |
| **Vivid scenario** | "Picture this: [specific relatable moment]" | "It's 9pm. You're rewriting the same email for the fourth time." |
| **Direct question** | "[Question the reader is already asking]" | "Why does your content rank but never convert?" |
| **"What if..."** | "What if [desirable outcome] without [painful constraint]?" | "What if your onboarding ran itself — no Zapier, no ops hire?" |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Surprising statistics and contrarian claims earn higher click-through on social; vivid scenarios and direct questions perform better in email — because email readers are in a passive reading state, not a skimming/scanning one.

## Subheading Patterns

**The rule:** Every H2 should be a complete thought, not a label.

| Weak (label) | Strong (complete thought) |
|---|---|
| "Background" | "This problem started in 2019 — and nobody noticed" |
| "Our Process" | "We interview every customer twice before writing a word" |
| "Results" | "Revenue grew 40% in 90 days — here's the breakdown" |
| "How We Built It" | "We rebuilt the engine in 3 days — here's what we learned" |
| "Benefits" | "Three things that change once you stop writing to rank" |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Complete-thought subheadings improve time-on-page because they function as a secondary reading layer — readers who skim H2s should be able to reconstruct the argument from subheadings alone.

**Test:** Read only your H2s in sequence. Does the story still hold? If not, rewrite.

## Proof Point Types

Use multiple proof types in one piece. Single-type proof feels thin.

| Type | Best used when | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Data / statistics** | You need to establish scope or urgency | "Teams using async updates report 30% fewer status meetings." |
| **Case study** | Reader needs to see themselves in the outcome | "Here's how [Company] cut churn from 8% to 2% in one quarter." |
| **Expert quote** | You need external credibility for a claim | "'The biggest mistake is optimising before you have signal.' — [Name, Title]" |
| **Personal anecdote** | You want to build trust and voice | "I spent 6 months doing this wrong before a founder call changed my view." |
| **Comparison** | The improvement needs magnitude | "Before: 4 hours of prep per meeting. After: 15 minutes with a shared doc." |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` A proof sandwich — open with data (scope), middle with case study (specificity), close with comparison (magnitude) — is the most persuasive proof structure for long-form B2B content.

## CTA Patterns

| Rule | Weak | Strong |
|------|------|--------|
| Single action per page | Three buttons: "Learn More", "Try Free", "Talk to Sales" | One primary CTA, one secondary (anchor link) |
| Verb + outcome | "Submit" | "Get my free audit" |
| Verb + outcome | "Click here" | "Start your 14-day trial" |
| Verb + outcome | "Download" | "Download the full playbook (PDF)" |
| Match funnel stage | "Buy now" on an awareness blog post | "See how it works" on an awareness blog post |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Pages with a single primary CTA convert at 2–3× the rate of pages with three or more competing CTAs — the paradox of choice applies to buttons.

**CTA placement checklist:**
- [ ] Above the fold on landing pages (hero section)
- [ ] After each major proof point in long-form content
- [ ] End of every email
- [ ] Never buried in body copy without visual differentiation (button, not hyperlink)
