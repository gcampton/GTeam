### Outbound Prospecting

**Define the ICP before building any list.** A useful ICP excludes accounts — if it does not, it is a TAM slide, not a targeting filter. Document the ICP using this template:

```
FIRMOGRAPHIC FILTERS
- Industry verticals: 2-4 specific sectors (not "enterprise" or "mid-market")
- Revenue range or headcount band
- Geography (if relevant to the go-to-market motion)
- Technology stack requirements (what must they already run?)

BEHAVIORAL QUALIFIERS
- What business event makes this account a buyer right now?
- What pain does the product solve that they cannot workaround indefinitely?
- Who inside the org feels that pain most acutely (the person, not the title)?
- What does their current workaround look like?

DISQUALIFIERS (equally important as qualifiers)
- What makes an account look good on paper but never close?
- Industries or segments where win rate is below 15%
- Company stages where the product is premature or overkill
```

**Tier accounts before assigning effort.** Match depth of engagement to likelihood of close and deal value:

- **Tier 1 (Top 50–100 accounts):** Full account research — annual reports, earnings calls, press releases, leadership changes. Multi-thread across 3–5 contacts (economic buyer, champion, influencer, end user). Custom messaging per persona referencing account-specific initiatives. Weekly account strategy review.
- **Tier 2 (Next 200–500 accounts):** Industry-specific messaging with account-level personalisation in the opening line. 2–3 contacts per account. Signal-triggered sequence enrollment. Quarterly promotion/demotion review based on engagement.
- **Tier 3 (Remaining ICP-fit):** Role- and industry-based sequences with dynamic tokens. Single primary contact. Signal-triggered enrollment only — no manual outreach without a signal.

**Source signals before writing a single word of outreach.** Outreach triggered by a buying signal converts 4–8x compared to untriggered cold outreach. Work signals in priority order:

- **Tier 1 — Active buying signals:** G2/review site visits, pricing page activity, competitor comparison content consumption, RFP or vendor evaluation announcements, job postings explicitly seeking to evaluate a technology category.
- **Tier 2 — Organisational change signals:** New leader in the buying persona's function (new VP = new priorities), funding events (Series B+ with stated growth goals = budget and urgency), hiring surges in the department the product serves, M&A activity (integration creates tool consolidation pressure).
- **Tier 3 — Technographic and behavioural signals:** Stack changes visible via BuiltWith or job postings, conference attendance or speaking on adjacent topics, content engagement (whitepaper downloads, webinar attendance, competitor content interaction), contract renewal timing where discoverable.

**Act on signals within 30 minutes.** After 24 hours a signal is stale. After 72 hours a competitor has already had the conversation.

**Design sequences with 8–12 touches over 3–4 weeks across channels.** Each touch must add a new value angle — repeating the same ask with different words is not a sequence.

```
Touch 1  (Day 1,  Email):    Signal-based opening + specific value prop + soft CTA
Touch 2  (Day 3,  LinkedIn): Connection request with personalised note (no pitch)
Touch 3  (Day 5,  Email):    Relevant insight or data point tied to their situation
Touch 4  (Day 8,  Phone):    Call with voicemail referencing email thread
Touch 5  (Day 10, LinkedIn): Engage with their content or share relevant content
Touch 6  (Day 14, Email):    Case study from analogous company/situation + clear CTA
Touch 7  (Day 17, Video):    60-second personalised Loom showing something specific to them
Touch 8  (Day 21, Email):    New angle — different pain point or stakeholder perspective
Touch 9  (Day 24, Phone):    Final call attempt
Touch 10 (Day 28, Email):    Breakup email — honest, brief, leave the door open
```

**Match channel to persona.** C-suite: LinkedIn InMail or warm intro first, short direct email second. VP-level: email primary, LinkedIn secondary, phone tertiary. Director: email, phone, LinkedIn. Manager/IC: email, LinkedIn, video (Loom). Technical buyers: email with technical content, community/Slack, LinkedIn.

**Cold email anatomy:**
- Subject line: 3–5 words, lowercase, looks like an internal message. References signal or specificity ("re: the new data team"). Never clickbait, never all-caps, never emoji.
- Opening line: signal-based and specific. "Saw you just hired 4 data engineers — scaling the analytics team usually means the current tooling is hitting its ceiling." Not "I hope this finds you well."
- Value proposition: one sentence in the buyer's language connecting their situation to an outcome they care about. Numbers and timeframes beat adjectives.
- Social proof (optional): one line citing a similar company, metric, and timeframe. Only include when genuinely relevant.
- CTA: single, clear, low friction. "Worth a 15-minute conversation to see if this applies to your team?" Not "I'd love to schedule a 30-minute demo."

**Reply rate benchmarks by quality tier:**
- Generic untargeted outreach: 1–3%
- Role/industry personalised: 5–8%
- Signal-based with account research: 12–25%
- Warm introduction or referral: 30–50%

**Track what matters:** signal-to-contact time (target <30 min), reply rate, positive reply rate, reply-to-meeting conversion (target 40–60% of positive replies), Stage 1→Stage 2 conversion (target 50%+), sequence completion rate (target 80%+).

### Discovery Call

**Pre-call research (complete before the call, not during it).** Research that can be done in advance should never be asked on the call — every situation question that could have been Googled signals laziness to a senior buyer.

Find before the call:
- Company: recent press, earnings calls or investor updates, stated strategic priorities, technology stack (BuiltWith, job postings), headcount trends (LinkedIn)
- Contact: their tenure in role, recent posts or public statements, likely KPIs for their function, any shared connections or context
- Signal: the specific trigger that prompted outreach — understand it well enough to reference it precisely
- Competitive landscape: which alternatives they are likely aware of or currently using

**Call structure (30-minute model):**

**Open — set the upfront contract (2 minutes).** Eliminate ambiguity and establish permission to ask hard questions before the first question is asked.

> "Thanks for making time. Here's what I was thinking for our 30 minutes: I'd love to ask some questions to understand what's going on in your world and whether there's a fit. You should ask me anything — I'll be direct. At the end, one of three things will happen: we'll both see a fit and schedule a next step, we'll realise this isn't the right solution and I'll say that honestly, or we'll need more information before we can decide. Any of those outcomes is fine. Does that work for you?"

**Explore — current state and pain (18 minutes, 60–70% of call time).** The most common discovery failure is rushing past pain to the pitch. Do not pitch until the buyer's situation can be articulated back to them better than they described it.

Opening territory question:
- Inbound: "What prompted you to take this call?"
- Outbound: "When I reached out, I mentioned [signal]. Can you tell me what's happening on your end with [topic]?"

Then follow the signal. Six things must be understood before the call ends:
1. **What is broken?** — stated in the buyer's words
2. **Why is it broken?** — root cause, not the surface symptom
3. **What does it cost?** — in dollars, time, risk, or people
4. **Who else cares?** — stakeholder map, who else feels this pain
5. **Why now?** — what changed that makes this a priority today
6. **What happens if they do nothing?** — the status quo has a price

**SPIN framework for uncovering pain depth:**
- **Situation questions** (2–3 maximum — do your homework first): "Walk me through how your team currently handles [process]." "What tools are you using for [function] today?"
- **Problem questions** (surface dissatisfaction): "Where does that process break down?" "What's the most frustrating part of how this works today?"
- **Implication questions** (this is where urgency is built — use them): "When that breaks down, what's the downstream impact on [related team or metric]?" "If that continues for another 6–12 months, what does that cost?" "Who else in the organisation feels the effects of this?"
- **Need-payoff questions** (let the buyer articulate the value): "If you could solve that, what would it unlock for your team?" "What would it mean for your team if [problem] was no longer a factor?"

Implication questions are uncomfortable to ask. That discomfort is a feature — the buyer has not fully confronted the cost of the status quo until these questions are asked.

**Deepen — gap mapping.** Map the current state precisely: environment, problems, measurable impact (revenue, cost, risk, people), and root cause. Then map the desired future state: what does "solved" look like in specific measurable terms, what metrics change and by how much, what becomes possible that is not possible today. The gap between current and future state is the sale. The bigger and more precisely mapped the gap, the more urgency. If the buyer can close the gap without the product, there is no deal.

**Test pitch — only what is relevant (6 minutes).** After understanding the buyer's situation, present the solution mapped directly to their stated problems. Not a product tour. Not the standard deck.

> "Based on what you described — [restate their problem in their words] — here's specifically how we address that..."

Limit to 2–3 capabilities that directly map to their stated pain. Resist showing everything the product can do.

**Close next step (4 minutes).** Define exactly what happens next — who does what, by when. Identify who else needs to be involved and why. Set the next meeting before ending this one. Agree on what a "no" looks like so neither side wastes time.

**What qualifies a discovery as successful:**
- The buyer says "that's a great question" and pauses to think
- The buyer reveals something they did not plan to share
- The buyer starts selling internally before being asked to
- The buyer's situation can be articulated back to them and they say "exactly"
- The buyer asks "so how would you solve this?" — they pitched themselves
- All six must-know items above are answered before the call ends

**Signs a discovery was rushed:**
- Pitching before minute 15
- Buyer giving one-word answers
- The buyer's personal stake in solving this is unknown
- Unable to explain why this is a priority now vs. six months from now
- Call ends without knowing who else is involved in the decision

**Coaching principles for discovery quality:**
- The buyer should talk 60% of the time or more. If talking more than 40%, pitching has begun.
- After asking a hard question, wait. The first answer is the surface answer. The answer after the pause is the real one.
- Qualify out fast. A deal with no real pain, no access to power, and no compelling timeline is a forecast lie — not a deal.

### Deal Qualification (MEDDPICC)

**Every active deal must be scored against all eight elements.** A deal without all eight answered is a deal that is not understood. Organisations fully adopting MEDDPICC report 18% higher win rates and 24% larger deal sizes — but only when used as a thinking tool, not a checkbox.

**M — Metrics.** The quantifiable business outcome the buyer needs to achieve. Not "they want better reporting" — that is a feature request. Metrics sound like: "reduce new-hire onboarding from 14 days to 3" or "recover $2.4M annually in revenue leakage from billing errors." If the buyer cannot articulate the metric, they have not built internal justification.

Diagnostic questions:
- "What does success look like in measurable terms 12 months from now?"
- "How is the business currently measuring the cost of this problem?"
- "What metric would change most if this were solved?"

**E — Economic Buyer.** The person who controls budget and can say yes when everyone else says no. Not the person who signs the PO — the person who decides the money gets spent. Test: can this person reallocate budget from another initiative to fund this? If no, the economic buyer has not been found.

Diagnostic questions:
- "Who ultimately owns the budget for an initiative like this?"
- "If you brought this to your leadership team tomorrow, who would need to approve it?"
- "Has budget been identified, or does it need to be justified from scratch?"

**D — Decision Criteria.** The specific technical, business, and commercial criteria the buyer will use to evaluate options. If guessing at the criteria, the competitor who helped write them is already winning. Influence criteria toward differentiators before the RFP lands.

Diagnostic questions:
- "What does your evaluation framework look like — what are the top 3–5 things you're scoring vendors on?"
- "How are those criteria weighted relative to each other?"
- "Who helped design the evaluation criteria?"

**D — Decision Process.** The actual sequence of steps from initial evaluation to signed contract — who is involved at each stage, what approvals are required, what timeline the buyer is working against. Every unmapped step is a place the deal can die silently.

Diagnostic questions:
- "Walk me through what happens between choosing a vendor and going live."
- "Who else needs to be involved in this decision that we have not spoken to yet?"
- "What does the approval chain look like above your level?"

**P — Paper Process.** Legal review, procurement, security questionnaire, vendor risk assessment, data processing agreements — the operational gauntlet where verbally-won deals go to die. A 6-week procurement cycle discovered in week 11 kills the quarter.

Diagnostic questions:
- "Has your legal team reviewed agreements like ours before? What does that typically look like?"
- "What does security review involve, and who runs it?"
- "Once a vendor is selected, what does the contracting process look like end-to-end?"

**I — Identify Pain.** The specific, quantified business problem driving the initiative. Pain is not "we need a better tool." Pain is: "We lost three enterprise deals last quarter because our implementation timeline was 90 days and the buyer chose a competitor who does it in 30." Pain has a cost — in revenue, risk, time, or reputation. If the cost of inaction cannot be quantified, the deal has no urgency and will stall.

Diagnostic questions:
- "What is the business cost of leaving this problem unsolved for another year?"
- "Who is most affected by this problem, and what does it cost them?"
- "What have you tried to fix it, and why didn't it work?"

**C — Champion.** An internal advocate who has power (organisational influence), access (to the economic buyer and decision-making process), and personal motivation (their career benefits from this initiative succeeding). A friendly contact who takes calls is not a champion. Test the champion: ask them to do something hard. If they will not, they are a coach at best.

Diagnostic questions:
- "Who internally is most invested in this initiative succeeding?"
- "If we needed to get 30 minutes with [economic buyer], how would that happen?"
- "Who stands to benefit most — personally — if this gets done?"

**C — Competition.** Every deal has competition: direct competitors, adjacent products expanding scope, internal build teams, or the most dangerous competitor — do nothing. Map the competitive field early. Categorise criteria into winning zones (differentiators the buyer values — amplify these), battling zones (both vendors credible — shift to TCO, implementation speed, ecosystem), and losing zones (competitor genuinely stronger — reposition, do not attack).

Diagnostic questions:
- "Are you evaluating other vendors alongside us?"
- "Have you worked with [competitor] before? What was that experience like?"
- "What would have to be true for you to decide to build this internally?"

**Deal health scoring — one status per element:**

| Status | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Green | Fully populated, validated, no gaps |
| Yellow | Partially known, assumption present, needs follow-up |
| Red | Unknown, unaddressed, or actively at risk |

**Deals with fewer than 5 of 8 MEDDPICC elements at Green or Yellow are underqualified.** Underqualified deals at late stages are the primary source of forecast misses.

**Running a deal review.** Work through these questions for every deal above threshold:
- "What changed since last week?" — momentum or stall
- "When did you last speak to the economic buyer?" — access or assumption
- "What does the champion say happens next?" — coaching or silence
- "Who else is the buyer evaluating?" — competitive awareness or blind spot
- "What happens if they do nothing?" — urgency or convenience
- "What is the paper process and has it started?" — timeline reality
- "What specific event is driving the timeline?" — compelling event or artificial deadline

**Red flags that kill deals:**
- Single-threaded to one contact who is not the economic buyer
- No compelling event or consequence of inaction
- Champion who will not grant access to the economic buyer
- Decision criteria that map perfectly to a competitor's strengths
- "We just need to see a demo" with no discovery completed
- Procurement timeline unknown or undiscussed
- Buyer initiated contact but cannot articulate the business problem

### Proposal & RFP Response

**Write the executive summary first.** It forces clarity on the argument before details proliferate. Many evaluators — especially senior stakeholders — read only this section. It is the proposal's closing argument, placed first.

Executive summary structure:
1. Mirror the buyer's situation in their own language (2–3 sentences proving the problem was heard and understood)
2. Introduce the central tension — the cost of inaction or the opportunity at risk, quantified
3. Present the solution thesis — how the approach resolves the tension (win themes appear here)
4. Offer proof — one or two concrete evidence points (metrics from similar engagements, differentiators)
5. Close with the transformed state — the specific outcome they can expect, tied to their stated goals

Keep the executive summary to one page. Every sentence must earn its place.

**Develop 3–5 win themes before writing any section.** Win themes are not slogans — they are the narrative backbone woven through every section of the document. A strong win theme:
- Names the buyer's specific challenge, not a generic industry problem
- Connects a concrete capability to a measurable outcome
- Differentiates without mentioning a competitor
- Is provable with evidence, case studies, or methodology

Weak: "We have deep experience in digital transformation."
Strong: "Our migration framework reduces cutover risk by staging critical workloads in parallel — the same approach that kept [client] at 99.97% uptime during a 14-month platform transition."

**Stress-test every win theme:** Is it specific to this buyer? Is it provable? Does it differentiate? Would a competitor struggle to claim the same thing?

**Proposal structure — problem before product:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Executive Summary | Closing argument placed first — business case, not capabilities |
| Problem Statement | Demonstrate understanding of the buyer's world in their language |
| Business Impact | Quantify the cost of the current state and the value of solving it |
| Proposed Solution | Methodology mapped directly to stated problems, not a feature list |
| Proof | Case studies and metrics from analogous situations — micro-stories, 2–4 sentences |
| Commercial Terms | Anchored on outcomes delivered, not cost incurred — ROI case built before the number appears |
| Next Steps | Explicit: who does what, by when, to move to contract |

**Content quality rules:**
- No empty adjectives. "Robust," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," and "world-class" are noise. Replace with specifics.
- Every claim needs evidence: a metric, a case study reference, a methodology detail, or a named framework.
- Graphics and visuals must advance the argument — every diagram should have a takeaway a skimmer can absorb in five seconds.
- Win themes must appear in the executive summary, solution narrative, case studies, and pricing rationale. Isolated themes are invisible themes.
- Pricing comes after value. Build the ROI case and establish the value of the approach before the buyer sees a number.

**Competitive positioning in proposals:** Never directly criticise competitors. Frame strengths as direct benefits that create contrast organically. Evaluators notice negative positioning and it erodes trust.

**For RFPs — compliance matrix first, then narrative.** Build a compliance matrix mapping every requirement to a specific section and response before writing prose. This ensures zero compliance gaps — then layer strategic context and win themes on top of each compliant answer. Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.

**Handling objections in the proposal:** Anticipate the top 3 objections the buyer will have after reading (price, switching cost, timeline risk) and address each preemptively in the relevant section, using the same evidence-based framing used elsewhere. Do not wait for the objection to surface in follow-up.

**Proposal review test:** If the buyer's name, challenges, and context could be swapped for another client without changing the content, the proposal is already losing.

### Pipeline Management

**Stage definitions with exit criteria and close probability:**

| Stage | Name | Exit Criteria | Close % |
|-------|------|---------------|---------|
| 1 | Prospecting | ICP-fit confirmed, signal identified, first outreach sent | 5% |
| 2 | Discovery | Discovery call completed, business pain documented, next step agreed | 20% |
| 3 | Qualification | MEDDPICC ≥5/8 elements Green or Yellow, economic buyer identified | 35% |
| 4 | Evaluation | Solution presented, evaluation criteria known, champion confirmed | 50% |
| 5 | Proposal | Written proposal or commercial terms submitted, procurement process mapped | 70% |
| 6 | Negotiation | Commercial terms under active negotiation, legal/procurement engaged | 85% |

**Pipeline velocity — the compound metric that drives forecasting:**

`Pipeline Velocity = (Qualified Opportunities × Average Deal Size × Win Rate) ÷ Sales Cycle Length`

Each variable is a diagnostic lever:
- **Qualified opportunities:** Declining top-of-funnel shows up in revenue 2–3 quarters later — the earliest warning signal in the system.
- **Average deal size:** Trending down may indicate discounting pressure or segment drift. Always segment — blended averages hide problems.
- **Win rate:** Track by stage, by segment, and over time. Stage-level win rates reveal where deals actually die. Declining win rates at a specific stage point to a process failure, not an individual performance issue.
- **Sales cycle length:** Lengthening cycles are often the first symptom of competitive pressure, buyer committee expansion, or qualification gaps.

**Pipeline coverage targets:**
- Mature, predictable business: 3x quota remaining
- Growth-stage or new market: 4–5x
- New rep ramping: 5x+

Coverage alone is insufficient. Quality-adjust by deal health score and stage age. A $5M pipeline with 20 stale, unqualified deals is worth less than a $2M pipeline with 8 active, well-qualified opportunities.

**Weekly pipeline review format (deal-level, not aggregate):**

For each deal under review:
1. What changed since last review? (Progress, not activity)
2. Who are conversations with? (Multi-threaded or single-threaded?)
3. What is the business case? (Can the buyer's reason to spend be articulated?)
4. What is the decision process? (Steps, people, criteria, timeline — all mapped?)
5. What is the biggest risk? (And what is the plan to mitigate it?)
6. What is the specific next step? (With a date, an owner, and a purpose)

**Forecast categories:**

| Category | Definition | Confidence |
|----------|------------|------------|
| Commit | Evidence-based: buyer has confirmed decision, timeline, and budget; no blocking unknowns | >90% |
| Best Case | Commit + high-velocity qualified deals where all MEDDPICC elements are Yellow or Green | >60% |
| Pipeline | Best Case + earlier-stage deals with validated pain and a clear path to decision | <60% |

Never present a single forecast number without a confidence range. Report at all three levels every review cycle.

**Identifying and saving at-risk deals.** A deal is at risk when any of the following are true:
- No activity logged in the last 14 days at Stage 4 or later
- Single-threaded to a contact who is not the economic buyer
- Stage has not changed in more than 1.5× the median stage duration
- Champion has gone silent or stopped responding
- Competitor has been given access that has not been matched
- Paper process has not been initiated with fewer than 6 weeks to close date

**Intervention sequence for at-risk deals:**
1. Diagnose the specific gap using MEDDPICC (which element is Red?)
2. Identify the single action that would most reduce risk (not five actions — one)
3. Assign an owner and a deadline for that action
4. Set a re-review checkpoint within 7 days
5. If gap does not close within agreed timeframe, move the deal to nurture or disqualify

**Pipeline hygiene rules:**
- Deals with no activity in 30+ days must be reviewed and either progressed or removed
- Close dates must be updated to reflect reality — not the date that makes the quarter look good
- Deals older than 2× the average sales cycle length should be flagged automatically for review
- Stage probability is not a forecast methodology — velocity, engagement, and MEDDPICC score drive the real number

### Lead Research & Prospecting

**Use when:** Building a prospect list, researching target accounts, or preparing for outreach.

**Step 1 — Define Ideal Customer Profile (if not already done):**
Reference the ICP section earlier in this methodology. If ICP already exists, skip to Step 2.

**Step 2 — Identify target companies:**

Research signals that indicate a company needs your solution:
- **Hiring signals:** Job postings for roles your product replaces or supports (WebSearch: `site:linkedin.com/jobs "[role] [industry]"`)
- **Technology signals:** Companies using complementary or competing tools (WebSearch: `"[competitor name] alternative" OR "[tech stack] users"`)
- **Growth signals:** Recent funding, expansion announcements, new office locations
- **Pain signals:** Forum complaints, negative reviews of competitors, support community posts
- **Trigger events:** Leadership changes, M&A activity, regulatory changes affecting their industry

**Step 3 — Score and prioritise leads:**

| Factor | Weight | Score 1-10 | Criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| ICP Fit | 30% | | Industry, size, geography match |
| Need Signal | 25% | | Evidence of the problem you solve |
| Budget Indicators | 20% | | Funding, revenue, existing spend on similar |
| Timing | 15% | | Trigger event recency, urgency signals |
| Accessibility | 10% | | Can you reach the decision maker? |

**Composite score** = weighted average. Focus outreach on scores ≥ 7.

**Step 4 — Enrich each lead:**

For each prioritised lead, gather:
- **Company:** name, website, industry, employee count, funding stage, revenue estimate
- **Decision maker:** name, title, LinkedIn URL, recent posts/activity
- **Context:** relevant news, blog posts, product launches, pain points mentioned publicly
- **Value prop:** specific to THIS company — how your product solves THEIR problem (not generic)

**Step 5 — Craft personalised outreach:**

For each lead, prepare:
- **Opening hook:** reference something specific about them (recent post, company news, shared connection)
- **Problem statement:** their specific pain point (not your product pitch)
- **Bridge:** how you've helped similar companies (social proof with specifics)
- **CTA:** low-commitment next step (15-min call, case study, free audit — NOT "buy now")

```markdown
**Website:** [URL]
**Industry:** [X] | **Size:** [X employees] | **Funding:** [X]

**Why they're a fit:**
- [Specific reason 1]
- [Specific reason 2]

**Value prop for them:**
[One sentence: how your product solves their specific problem]

**Outreach approach:**
- Hook: [What to reference]
- Channel: [Email / LinkedIn / warm intro]
- CTA: [Specific ask]
```

**Cadence:** Build 10-20 leads per week. Review and refresh monthly — companies' situations change. Track outreach results in a pipeline (sent → replied → meeting → proposal → closed).

### Sales Coaching

**Use when:** Developing sales reps, reviewing pipeline quality, or preparing for important deals. Distinct from executing sales — this is about making sellers better.

**Pipeline review framework:**

For each deal in pipeline, assess:

| Dimension | Question | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Champion | Do we have an internal advocate? | No named champion |
| Decision process | Do we know how they decide? | "They'll just decide" |
| Timeline | Is there a compelling event? | No deadline or urgency |
| Budget | Is budget allocated? | "They'll find budget" |
| Competition | Who else are they evaluating? | "I don't think anyone" |
| Next step | Is there a concrete next action? | "I'll follow up next week" |

Any deal with 3+ red flags needs intervention before it stalls.

**Call review methodology:**

After a sales call, assess:
1. **Discovery quality:** Did the rep uncover the real problem, or accept surface-level answers?
2. **Talk-to-listen ratio:** Target 30/70 (rep/prospect). > 50% rep talking = presenting, not discovering.
3. **Questions asked:** Open-ended vs closed. "Tell me about..." > "Do you have...?"
4. **Objection handling:** Did they acknowledge → probe → address? Or defend → dismiss?
5. **Next step:** Was a specific next action agreed with a date? Not "I'll send you some info."

**Coaching conversation structure (for 1:1s):**
1. **Ask first:** "How do you think that call went?" (self-assessment before feedback)
2. **Specific observation:** "I noticed when they said X, you responded with Y" (not "you need to listen better")
3. **Impact:** "The effect was Z — they moved on without answering your question"
4. **Alternative:** "What if you'd said [specific alternative]? What might have happened?"
5. **Practice:** Role-play the scenario with the improved approach
6. **One thing:** End with ONE focus area for next week, not five

**Rep development plan template:**

| Skill Area | Current Level | Evidence | Development Action | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discovery | [1-5] | [specific observation] | [specific practice] | [date] |
| Objection handling | [1-5] | [specific observation] | [specific practice] | [date] |
| Closing | [1-5] | [specific observation] | [specific practice] | [date] |
