# Key Financial Ratios

> **Confidence:** All benchmarks are `[HYPOTHESIS]` (reasoned industry averages). Healthy ranges vary significantly by industry, business model, and stage — always compare to sector peers and prior periods, not just these thresholds.

---

## How to Use This File

1. Calculate the ratio from the financial statements provided.
2. Compare to the healthy range — note this is a starting point, not a definitive pass/fail.
3. Look at the trend (3+ periods) — deteriorating trend is more concerning than a single low reading.
4. When a ratio is outside range, go to "Fix Direction" and trace through to the underlying cause.

---

## Section 1 — Liquidity Ratios

Measure ability to meet short-term obligations.

| Name | Formula | Healthy Range | Warning Sign | Fix Direction |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| Current Ratio | Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities | 1.5–3.0 | < 1.0 (can't cover short-term liabilities) | Improve collections, reduce short-term debt, raise working capital facility |
| Quick Ratio (Acid Test) | (Current Assets − Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities | 0.8–1.5 | < 0.8 (reliant on selling inventory to pay debts) | Accelerate AR collection; defer non-critical payments |
| Cash Ratio | Cash & Equivalents ÷ Current Liabilities | 0.2–0.5 | < 0.1 (no cash buffer); > 1.0 (excess idle cash) | Below: secure credit facility. Above: deploy excess cash or return to shareholders |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Notes:
- Retail and manufacturing businesses naturally carry more inventory → quick ratio more informative than current ratio.
- SaaS / service businesses: current ratio often > 2 because they carry minimal inventory and have recurring revenue — context matters.
- A current ratio > 3 can indicate inefficient use of assets (too much cash sitting idle, not invested).

## Section 2 — Profitability Ratios

Measure how efficiently revenue converts to profit.

| Name | Formula | Healthy Range | Warning Sign | Fix Direction |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| Gross Margin | (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100 | Varies by industry (see below) | Declining trend; below industry average | Review COGS breakdown; renegotiate supplier contracts; reassess pricing |
| EBITDA Margin | EBITDA ÷ Revenue × 100 | 10–30% (general); varies widely | < 10% (thin margin); negative (burning cash on operations) | Cut operating costs; improve gross margin; review SG&A efficiency |
| Net Profit Margin | Net Profit After Tax ÷ Revenue × 100 | 5–20% (general) | < 0% (loss-making) | Full P&L review; pricing, cost, and tax structure |
| Return on Equity (ROE) | Net Profit After Tax ÷ Shareholders' Equity × 100 | 15–25% | < 10% (poor return on capital) | Improve profitability or reduce equity base (dividends, buybacks) |
| Return on Assets (ROA) | Net Profit After Tax ÷ Total Assets × 100 | 5–15% | < 5% (assets not generating returns) | Improve margin or dispose of underperforming assets |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Gross Margin by Industry Type:

| Industry | Typical Gross Margin |
|----------|---------------------|
| SaaS / Software | 60–80% |
| Professional Services | 30–60% |
| E-commerce / Retail | 20–50% |
| Manufacturing | 20–40% |
| Construction | 15–25% |
| Grocery / FMCG | 5–25% |
| Hospitality | 30–40% |

A gross margin significantly below industry average warrants immediate investigation: pricing too low, supplier costs too high, or undetected COGS misclassification.

## Section 3 — Efficiency Ratios

Measure how effectively assets and liabilities are managed.

| Name | Formula | Healthy Range | Warning Sign | Fix Direction |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) | (AR ÷ Revenue) × Number of Days in Period | 30–45 days (B2B); lower is better | > 60 days (slow collections); rising trend | Tighten credit terms; chase overdue invoices; offer early payment discounts |
| Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) | (AP ÷ COGS) × Number of Days in Period | 30–60 days | < 20 days (paying too fast, losing working capital); > 90 days (strain on supplier relationships) | Optimise payment timing; negotiate better terms with suppliers |
| Inventory Turnover | COGS ÷ Average Inventory | 4–12× per year (varies widely) | < 4× (slow-moving stock, obsolescence risk); high and rising may signal understocking | Review inventory levels; identify slow-moving SKUs; adjust reorder points |
| Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) | (Average Inventory ÷ COGS) × Number of Days in Period | 30–90 days (product businesses) | > 90 days (excess/obsolete stock) | Clearance of slow-moving stock; reduce safety stock levels |
| Asset Turnover | Revenue ÷ Total Assets | 0.5–2.0× | Low and declining (assets not generating revenue) | Improve revenue per asset; dispose of underperforming assets |
| Revenue per Employee | Revenue ÷ FTE Headcount | Varies by industry | Declining trend (productivity falling) | Workforce review; automation; revenue growth strategy |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC):

```
CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO
```

- Positive CCC = company funds its own working capital (cash tied up in operations) — typical for manufacturers
- Negative CCC = suppliers and customers fund the business (e.g. Amazon, Costco) — cash-generative model
- Rising CCC over time = working capital deteriorating — investigate each component

## Section 4 — Leverage (Solvency) Ratios

Measure long-term financial stability and debt burden.

| Name | Formula | Healthy Range | Warning Sign | Fix Direction |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| Debt-to-Equity (D/E) | Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity | 0.5–2.0 (general); varies by industry | > 2.0 (heavily leveraged); negative equity (technical insolvency) | Reduce debt; raise equity; improve retained earnings |
| Debt-to-EBITDA | Total Debt ÷ EBITDA | < 3.0 (investment grade); < 5.0 (acceptable growth co.) | > 5.0 (high leverage); > 6.0 (distress territory) | Debt repayment plan; improve EBITDA; refinance at lower rates |
| Interest Coverage | EBIT ÷ Interest Expense | > 3.0 | < 1.5 (earnings barely cover interest — HIGH risk); < 1.0 (cannot service debt from operations) | Refinance to reduce interest; improve EBIT; raise equity |
| Net Debt | Total Debt − Cash & Equivalents | Context-dependent | High net debt + low interest coverage = danger | Detailed cash flow review + debt maturity schedule |
| Equity Ratio | Shareholders' Equity ÷ Total Assets × 100 | 30–60% | < 20% (thin equity cushion) | Retain earnings; reduce liabilities; raise equity |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Notes:
- Capital-intensive industries (property, utilities, manufacturing) routinely carry D/E > 2.0 — compare to sector.
- Tech/SaaS companies often carry minimal debt — a D/E > 1.0 in SaaS warrants explanation.
- Interest coverage < 2.0 is a common covenant trigger in bank loan agreements — flag if approaching.

## Section 5 — Growth & Valuation Metrics

For growth-stage or investment-context reviews.

| Name | Formula | Healthy Range | Warning Sign | Fix Direction |
|------|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| Revenue Growth Rate | (Current Revenue − Prior Revenue) ÷ Prior Revenue × 100 | > 20% (early stage); > 10% (mature) | Declining growth rate in early-stage company | Demand analysis; product-market fit review |
| MRR / ARR Growth (SaaS) | Month-end MRR / ARR vs prior period | > 10% MoM (early); > 100% YoY (growth stage) | Flat or declining MRR | Churn analysis; expansion revenue strategy |
| Net Revenue Retention (NRR) | (Opening MRR + Expansion − Contraction − Churn) ÷ Opening MRR × 100 | > 100% (best in class > 120%) | < 100% (customer base shrinking in revenue terms) | Reduce churn; increase expansion/upsell |
| Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) | Sales & Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers Acquired | Depends on LTV; LTV:CAC > 3:1 | LTV:CAC < 1:1 (losing money on each customer) | Reduce CAC or increase LTV (price, retention, upsell) |
| Lifetime Value (LTV) | Average Revenue per Account × Gross Margin % × Average Customer Lifespan | > 3× CAC | LTV declining (churn rising or pricing eroding) | Retention programmes; pricing review |
| Burn Multiple | Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR | < 1.0 (efficient); < 2.0 (acceptable) | > 2.0 (burning too much to grow); > 3.0 (concerning) | Reduce burn rate or improve ARR growth efficiency |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` LTV:CAC guidance:
- < 1:1 — unsustainable; losing money on customer acquisition
- 1–3:1 — borderline; need efficiency improvements
- > 3:1 — healthy; good unit economics
- > 5:1 — potentially under-investing in growth

## Quick-Reference: Ratio → What It Tells You → When to Flag

| Ratio | Tells You | Flag When |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Current Ratio | Can pay short-term bills? | < 1.0 |
| Quick Ratio | Liquid enough without selling stock? | < 0.8 |
| Gross Margin | Pricing/cost health | Declining trend or below industry average |
| EBITDA Margin | Operating efficiency | < 10% or negative |
| DSO | How fast customers pay | > 60 days or rising |
| DPO | How long you take to pay | > 90 days (supplier pressure) or < 20 days (losing working capital) |
| D/E | Debt load relative to equity | > 2.0 (most industries) |
| Interest Coverage | Can service debt from earnings? | < 2.0 |
| Debt/EBITDA | Years of earnings needed to repay debt | > 5.0 |
| NRR (SaaS) | Revenue retention from existing customers | < 100% |
| Burn Multiple | Capital efficiency of growth | > 2.0 |
