# Google E-E-A-T Guidelines

> **Confidence:** All recommendations are `[HYPOTHESIS]` unless marked `[TESTED: date]` or `[REVISED: date]`. Check `../results/` for empirical outcomes before applying advice.

E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) is the framework Google's quality raters use to evaluate content quality. It is not a direct ranking signal, but it shapes the algorithms that are. High E-E-A-T content gets rewarded; low E-E-A-T content gets suppressed — especially for YMYL topics.

---

## The Four Pillars

### Experience
Does the content creator have **firsthand, real-life experience** with the subject?

- Tried the product themselves
- Visited the location
- Performed the task being described
- Lived through the situation

**Why it matters:** Google can increasingly detect generic AI-written content that describes things accurately but without genuine first-person experience. Real experience produces specific, unexpected details that templated content lacks.

### Expertise
Does the author possess **verified knowledge, skills, or credentials** in the topic?

- Formal qualifications (medical degree, legal bar, financial licence)
- Demonstrated track record (years of published work, case studies, results)
- Specialist knowledge evidenced in the content itself (not just claimed in the bio)

**Why it matters:** For YMYL topics, Google expects content to be written by someone who could be held professionally accountable for it.

### Authoritativeness
Is the **website or author** a recognised, go-to source for this topic?

- External sites and publications link to this site as a reference
- The author is cited or quoted elsewhere in the niche
- The brand has a Wikipedia article, industry listings, or press coverage
- Other authoritative sites mention the brand without being asked

**Why it matters:** Authority is largely external validation — it can't be self-declared. It accumulates through consistent output, link building, and reputation management over time.

### Trustworthiness
Is the **page accurate, secure, and transparent**? This is the most critical pillar.

- HTTPS (non-negotiable)
- Accurate, up-to-date information with clear sourcing
- No hidden authorship or anonymous "editorial team" for YMYL content
- Clear privacy policy, contact information, and terms
- No deceptive design patterns or misleading claims
- Regular content updates — outdated information is a trust signal failure

**Why it matters:** Without trust, the other three pillars don't convert. A site can have experienced, expert, authoritative authors and still rank poorly if it appears untrustworthy.

## YMYL Topics — Higher E-E-A-T Bar

YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) topics require stricter E-E-A-T because poor content in these areas can cause real harm.

**YMYL categories:**
- Health and medical advice
- Financial advice, investment, tax
- Legal guidance
- Safety and emergency information
- News and current events
- Civic and government information

**For YMYL content:**
- Author must have verifiable credentials in the field
- Content must be reviewed and updated when facts change
- Sources must be cited (studies, official bodies, credentialed experts)
- Publish date and last-reviewed date must be visible
- Author bio must include relevant qualifications, not just job title

## How to Demonstrate E-E-A-T

### Author Bios
Every piece of content should have a visible author with:
- Full name (not "Editorial Team" or anonymous)
- Relevant credentials or experience for the topic
- Links to their other published work or professional profiles
- Photo (builds human trust signal)

For YMYL: credentials must be verifiable (link to LinkedIn, professional registration, or institutional affiliation).

### Firsthand Experience Signals
- Original photos (not stock) — especially for product reviews, location guides, how-tos
- Video demonstrating the process or result
- Case studies with real numbers and outcomes
- Specific, unexpected details that only come from doing the thing ("the checkout process has a bug on mobile that means you need to tap twice on the submit button")
- Personal anecdotes with dates, context, and specifics

### Citing Credible Sources
- Link to primary sources: studies, official bodies, government sites
- Cite statistics with the original source (not a blog that cites a blog that cites the study)
- Use recent sources — outdated citations undermine trust
- For medical/legal/financial: cite peer-reviewed research or official regulatory guidance where possible

### Building Authoritativeness Over Time
- Consistent publishing in one niche (generalist sites have lower authority per topic)
- Guest posting on authoritative sites in the niche (with genuine bylines)
- Getting quoted or referenced by journalists and industry publications
- Speaking at conferences or events in the field
- Building a clear topical authority map — own a topic cluster before branching out

### Site-Level Trust Signals
- HTTPS with valid certificate
- Visible contact page with real contact details
- Clear privacy policy and cookie policy
- Terms of service for commercial sites
- Physical address for local or e-commerce businesses
- Prominent "About" page explaining who runs the site and why

## E-E-A-T in Content Creation Workflow

When writing or rewriting a page, evaluate against these questions before publishing:

**Experience check:**
- [ ] Does this content include specific firsthand details, not just generic information?
- [ ] Are there original examples, photos, or case studies from real use?
- [ ] Would someone who hasn't done this thing be able to write exactly this content? (If yes, add more specifics.)

**Expertise check:**
- [ ] Is the author qualified to write this? Is that stated clearly?
- [ ] Are claims accurate and sourced?
- [ ] Is the depth appropriate — does it go beyond surface-level advice?

**Authoritativeness check:**
- [ ] Does the site have other strong content in this topic cluster?
- [ ] Are there internal links from related authoritative pages on this site?
- [ ] Is there any external validation (links, mentions) pointing to this page or site?

**Trustworthiness check:**
- [ ] Is the page on HTTPS?
- [ ] Is the publish/update date visible?
- [ ] Are all claims accurate? Are any outdated?
- [ ] Is the author clearly identified?
- [ ] Are sources linked?

## E-E-A-T for AI-Assisted Content

AI-generated content is not inherently penalised, but AI content that lacks E-E-A-T signals will underperform. When using AI to write or assist:

- Add firsthand experience the AI cannot fabricate (original data, personal anecdotes, real results)
- Have a credentialed human review YMYL content before publishing
- Ensure the published author is a real person who stands behind the content
- Fact-check all statistics and claims — AI hallucinates citations
- Add original images, not AI-generated stock
- Rewrite generic AI phrasing with specific, experienced-sounding language

The goal is not to hide AI assistance but to ensure the final output reflects genuine experience and expertise, not templated output.
