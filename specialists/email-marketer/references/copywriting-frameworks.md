# Email Copywriting Frameworks

> **Confidence:** All recommendations are `[HYPOTHESIS]` (untested best-practice) unless marked `[TESTED: date]` or `[REVISED: date]`. Check `../results/` for empirical outcomes before applying advice.

Email copy lives or dies in the inbox. The subject line earns the open; the opening line earns the next sentence. Every element below exists to earn the click — one click, one action.

---

## Subject Line Formulas

The subject line is the only thing that determines whether the email is read. Write it last, but test it first.

### Curiosity Gap

Create an information gap the reader needs to close. Don't explain — intrigue.

```
"You're making this mistake every morning"
"The thing nobody tells you about cold outreach"
"Why your open rates dropped last month"
```

**Rules:** Don't resolve the curiosity in the subject line. Leave the answer in the email body.

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Curiosity gap subject lines drive higher opens in cold email but suffer higher unsubscribe rates if the body doesn't deliver. The open rate is the vanity metric — click rate is the signal.

### Specificity

Specific numbers outperform vague benefits. The specificity signals that real data exists behind the claim.

```
"3 things I changed to hit $100k MRR"
"How we cut churn from 9% to 2.4% in 60 days"
"The 4-step sequence that books 12 demos a week"
```

**Rules:** Use actual numbers from real outcomes wherever possible. Invented specifics erode trust over time.

### Personalisation

`[First Name]` alone is table stakes and is becoming less effective as it's over-used. Contextual personalisation outperforms name-only.

```
"[First name], this one's for you"
"[First name], saw you work in [industry] — relevant question"
"[Company name]'s onboarding has one gap"
```

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Contextual personalisation (company, role, recent action) outperforms name-only personalisation by a significant margin in cold email — but requires data quality. A wrong personalisation token damages trust more than no personalisation.

### Benefit-Forward

State the transformation plainly. Works well for warm lists where the reader already trusts the sender.

```
"How to write faster emails in half the time"
"Get 3 hours back from your week — here's how"
"The framework that replaced all our strategy meetings"
```

**Rules:** The benefit must be specific and credible. "Get better at marketing" is not a benefit.

## Subject Line Quick-Reference

| Formula | Best for | Char target |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Curiosity gap | Cold email, re-engagement | 35–50 |
| Specificity | Case studies, results reveals | 40–55 |
| Personalisation | Cold outreach, segmented lists | 30–45 |
| Benefit-forward | Warm nurture, newsletters | 40–60 |

**Character target:** Aim for 35–50 characters to display fully on mobile (most email opens).

## Preview Text

The preview text is the second subject line. It appears in the inbox after the subject — 40–90 characters displayed depending on client and screen size.

**Rules:**
- Extends the subject, never repeats it
- Adds context or deepens the intrigue
- Treat it as the second hook, not a summary

| Subject | Weak preview text | Strong preview text |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| "You're making this mistake every morning" | "Read our latest email" | "It costs you 45 minutes a day without you noticing" |
| "3 things I changed to hit $100k MRR" | "Check out this month's newsletter" | "Number 2 took 20 minutes to implement" |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Preview text is treated as an afterthought by most email marketers. A/B testing preview text separately from subject lines is uncommon but high-leverage — it affects opens on mobile where preview text is most visible.

## Opening Lines — What Works vs. What Doesn't

The first sentence must earn the second sentence. There is no warmup.

**Don't open with:**

```
"I hope this email finds you well."
"My name is [X] and I work at [Y]."
"I wanted to reach out because..."
"We're excited to share..."
"As a valued customer..."
```

These are all writer-centric, not reader-centric. The reader does not care about your excitement.

**Open with the hook instead:**

```
"Your onboarding flow has one step that kills 30% of trials."
"Most founders discover this too late: [specific insight]."
"Something I noticed about [their company/situation]."
"[Specific number] of your competitors are already doing this."
"[Question they're already asking themselves]."
```

`[HYPOTHESIS]` The most effective cold email opening line makes a claim the reader immediately wants to verify as true or false. Curiosity is better than flattery.

## Email Body Structure

One structure for transactional and promotional emails. Deviations are intentional, not default.

```
[HOOK — 2 lines max]
The claim or question that earned the open. Don't restate the subject.

[CONTEXT — 2–3 sentences]
Why this matters now. The problem or opportunity in their world.
No pitch yet. This is where you show you understand their situation.

[OFFER — 1 paragraph]
The solution, the resource, the event, the product.
Benefits, not features. Outcome language: "so that you can..."
One offer. Not three options with equal emphasis.

[CTA — 1 button or bolded link]
Verb + specific outcome.
Visually distinct from body text.
```

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Emails with more than one primary CTA dilute click-through rate. If a second CTA is needed (e.g., forward this email), make it typographically subordinate — smaller, plain text link, not a second button.

## CTA Copy Rules

| Rule | Weak | Strong |
|------|------|--------|
| Verb + specific outcome | "Click here" | "Get the free template" |
| Verb + specific outcome | "Download" | "Download the 2025 benchmark report" |
| Verb + specific outcome | "Learn more" | "See how the integration works (2-min video)" |
| Single CTA per email | Two equal buttons | One primary button, one optional plain-text link |
| Match what happens next | "Start your trial" → lands on homepage | "Start your trial" → lands on signup form |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` "Learn more" is the weakest CTA across all email types. It signals the email hasn't yet made the case for why the reader should act. If "learn more" is the CTA, the offer section needs rewriting.

## Sequence Arc Patterns

Emails don't work in isolation. Each email should advance a relationship arc.

### Welcome Sequence (3–5 emails)

| Email | Purpose | Tone |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1 — Day 0 | Deliver the value promise; confirm they're in the right place | Warm, direct |
| 2 — Day 2 | Quick win: one actionable thing they can do now | Practical |
| 3 — Day 4 | Social proof: a customer story or specific outcome | Trust-building |
| 4 — Day 7 | Teach something non-obvious about their problem | Authority |
| 5 — Day 10 | Soft offer or next step | Low-pressure |

### Nurture Sequence (ongoing)

Purpose: education. The reader is not ready to buy. Don't push — teach.

- One insight or framework per email
- Primary CTA is content (article, video, tool) not a product link
- Cadence: weekly or biweekly — consistent, not random

### Conversion Sequence (3–5 emails)

Sent when a purchase decision window is open (launch, limited offer, trial expiry).

| Email | Focus |
|-------|-------|
| 1 — Open | Announce the offer + primary benefit |
| 2 — Middle | Overcome the top objection |
| 3 — Social proof | Customer story or result |
| 4 — Urgency | Deadline or scarcity (only if real) |
| 5 — Last call | Final reminder, close with empathy |

`[HYPOTHESIS]` Fake urgency ("limited time offer" with no real deadline) damages sender reputation and list trust more than skipping urgency altogether. Use deadline language only when a real deadline exists.

### Re-engagement Sequence (3 emails before unsubscribe)

For subscribers inactive for 90+ days:

```
Email 1: "Still interested?" — simple, no pressure, single question CTA
Email 2: "Here's what you missed" — value summary, no guilt
Email 3: "We're removing you tomorrow" — honest, clean opt-in CTA
→ No click: unsubscribe and move to suppression list
```

## P.S. Line

The P.S. is often the second-most-read element in an email (after the subject line), because readers frequently skim to the bottom before reading.

**Use the P.S. for one of:**
- Social proof: "P.S. [Company] reduced churn by 40% using this — their story here."
- Secondary CTA: "P.S. Not ready yet? Here's the case study to share with your team."
- Urgency signal: "P.S. The early-bird price ends Friday at midnight."
- Pattern interrupt: a personal, unexpected observation that reinforces the sender's voice

`[HYPOTHESIS]` A P.S. that delivers new value (not a repetition of the main CTA) outperforms a P.S. that simply restates "don't miss this." The goal is to give the skimmer a reason to scroll back up.
