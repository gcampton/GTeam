# Marketing Psychology & Persuasion Reference

Use this file to select the right psychological lever for the task at hand. Organized by application context, not by theory name.

## Quick-Reference: Challenge → Models to Apply

| Challenge | Primary models | Secondary models |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Low conversion rate | Hick's Law, BJ Fogg, Activation Energy, Status-Quo Bias | Default Effect, Zeigarnik Effect |
| Price objections | Anchoring, Framing, Mental Accounting, Loss Aversion | Rule of 100, Contrast Effect |
| Building trust with a cold audience | Authority Bias, Social Proof, Reciprocity, Pratfall Effect | Mere Exposure Effect, Unity Principle |
| Creating urgency without lying | Loss Aversion, Scarcity (genuine), Zeigarnik Effect | Goal-Gradient Effect, Hyperbolic Discounting |
| High churn / retention problems | Endowment Effect, Switching Costs, IKEA Effect, Status-Quo Bias | Peak-End Rule, Sunk Cost (avoiding) |
| Stalled growth | Theory of Constraints, Barbell Strategy, Feedback Loops, Compounding | Network Effects, Critical Mass |
| Decision paralysis (prospect won't buy) | Paradox of Choice, Default Effect, Nudge Theory | Door-in-the-Face, Foot-in-the-Door |
| Poor onboarding/activation | Goal-Gradient Effect, IKEA Effect, Commitment & Consistency | Zeigarnik Effect, BJ Fogg |
| Writing copy that converts | Jobs to be Done, Customer Language, Contrast Effect, Specificity Bias | Curse of Knowledge, Framing Effect |
| Ad creative that stops the scroll | Pattern Interrupt, Curiosity Gap, Availability Heuristic | Mimetic Desire, Social Proof |

## Buyer Psychology & Decision-Making

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
Customers don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. The job is functional ("send invoices faster"), emotional ("feel less anxious about money"), and social ("look competent to my team").

**Apply in copy:** Lead with the job, not the product. "Take back your Sunday evenings" beats "Automated reporting tool."

**Apply in CRO:** Audit whether the page headline matches the job the customer came to hire. Mismatches between the job in the customer's head and the headline cause immediate bounces.

### Switching Dynamics (JTBD Four Forces)
Four forces act on every purchasing decision simultaneously:

| Force | Direction | How to address |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| **Push** (dissatisfaction with status quo) | Toward buying | Amplify in the problem section — name the frustration specifically |
| **Pull** (attraction to the new solution) | Toward buying | Lead with the transformation, not features |
| **Anxiety** (fear of the new) | Away from buying | FAQ, guarantees, free trials, social proof near the CTA |
| **Habit** (pull back to old way) | Away from buying | Acknowledge it: "If you're used to spreadsheets..." then show the bridge |

### Availability Heuristic
People judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. A customer who just read about a data breach thinks security matters more than they did yesterday.

**Apply:** Time your messaging to relevant events (product launches, industry news, seasonality). Lead with problems that are "in the air" for your audience right now.

### Confirmation Bias
People seek information that confirms what they already believe. They've often decided before they consciously realise it.

**Apply in nurture:** Don't try to reverse a belief — align with it and redirect. "You're right that most [category] tools are overly complex. Here's how [Product] is different."

### Mimetic Desire
People want things because other people want them. Desire is socially contagious — we observe what others pursue and adopt those wants as our own.

**Apply:** Social proof isn't just trust-building. It actively creates desire. "Join 12,000 teams" doesn't just reduce anxiety — it creates wanting.

### Fundamental Attribution Error
We attribute others' behaviour to character, not circumstances. Customers think they failed at their old solution because they weren't trying hard enough, not because the tool was bad.

**Apply in copy:** Reframe failures as circumstantial, not personal. "It's not that you're bad at [task] — your tools make it harder than it needs to be." This is deeply relieving to read.

## Persuasion Principles (Cialdini + Extensions)

### Reciprocity
Give value first, unconditionally. People feel obligated to return favours — the obligation is genuine and persistent.

**Apply:** Free tools, ungated guides, templates, diagnostic tools. The more specific and valuable the gift, the stronger the reciprocity obligation.

**Caution:** Gifts that feel like manipulative previews of a pitch ("free webinar" that's a 60-minute sales pitch) reverse the effect — they trigger resentment.

### Commitment & Consistency
Once people take a small action, they're more likely to take the next. The first step activates identity ("I'm someone who does this").

**Apply in onboarding:** Design each step to be visibly completable. Progress bars, checklists, and "Step 2 of 4" trigger completion drive. The first action should require almost no effort — answering one question or connecting one integration.

**Apply in conversion:** Micro-commitments before the ask. "Take our 2-minute assessment" → results page → CTA converts higher than a cold CTA because the user has already invested.

### Social Proof
People look to others in uncertain situations. The more similar the other person, the stronger the influence.

**Specificity hierarchy (strongest to weakest):**
1. Named customer in the same role, company size, and industry with a specific result
2. Named customer with a specific result
3. Named customer without a specific result
4. Review count ("4.7/5 from 340 reviews")
5. Customer count ("12,000 teams")
6. Logos without context

**Apply:** Always match social proof to the audience segment. Enterprise prospects need enterprise logos. SMB teams need SMB testimonials.

### Authority Bias
People defer to credentialed experts, even in areas outside the expert's domain.

**Apply:** Feature specific credentials (not just "industry leaders"), research citations, published methodologies. Founder background matters when the founder's expertise is the product's differentiator.

**Caution:** Generic authority claims ("trusted by experts") are invisible. Specific credentials ("SOC 2 Type II certified") work.

### Scarcity & Urgency
Limited availability and time pressure increase perceived value and action rates. Works powerfully — and backfires catastrophically when fabricated.

**Rule:** Only use genuine scarcity. Fake countdown timers are detectable and destroy trust permanently with the portion of customers who notice.

**Real scarcity options:** Cohort-based onboarding ("next cohort: March 3"), capacity limits for white-glove plans, early-access pricing windows.

### Liking & Similarity
People buy from people they like. They like people who are similar to them.

**Apply in copy:** Use the audience's own language. Mirror their self-description. Founders writing in first person, acknowledging shared frustrations, converts better than corporate copy.

### Unity Principle (Cialdini)
Position the brand as part of the customer's tribe, not just a vendor. "We" is more persuasive than "you and us."

**Apply:** Insider language, reference to shared values, "join the community of [identity]." Works especially well in developer tools, professional communities, and niche verticals.

## Cognitive Biases in Pricing & Offers

### Anchoring Effect
The first number in a decision anchors all subsequent judgement. People evaluate "expensive or cheap" relative to the first number they see, not in absolute terms.

**Apply in pricing:** Show the highest tier first (left to right on pricing pages, or highest value first in a comparison). The Enterprise/Business price makes the Recommended tier feel reasonable.

**Apply in sales:** State the full value of what they're getting before the price. "This would normally take your team 40 hours..." before a $299/month offer repositions the price.

### Decoy Effect
Adding a clearly inferior third option makes the preferred option look obviously better by comparison.

**Apply in pricing tiers:** An "almost as good but less convenient" middle option (fewer features, same price as Recommended) makes the Recommended tier feel like it has obvious extra value. This is why the middle tier should have the "Best Value" badge.

### Framing Effect
Identical information, presented differently, feels different. "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are the same fact but feel opposite.

**Apply in copy:** "Save 5 hours every week" and "Stop losing 5 hours every week" are the same claim, but the loss framing consistently outperforms the gain framing for motivation to act.

**Apply in pricing:** "$1/day" and "$30/month" are the same price. Choose based on context: use smaller framing when cost anxiety is the main objection; use monthly/annual framing when budget cycles are the buying process.

### Mental Accounting
People categorise money differently depending on its perceived source and purpose. A "business expense" feels different from personal money even if it's the same amount.

**Apply:** Frame costs in the right account. For B2B: "replaces the cost of one manual task" or "pays for itself with one prevented mistake" positions spending against an existing budget line.

**Apply (Rule of 100):** For discounts under $100, use percentages ("save 30%"). For discounts over $100, use absolute numbers ("save $200"). Whichever feels larger wins.

### Loss Aversion
Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. "Don't lose" outperforms "gain" in most contexts.

**Apply in copy:** "Don't let your competitors get there first" often outperforms "Get ahead of your competitors." Test both — context matters (some audiences respond to positive framing more).

**Apply in CRO:** Address what the customer loses by NOT buying (the opportunity cost) not just what they gain by buying.

### Endowment Effect
People value things more once they own them (or feel they own them).

**Apply in trials:** Design the trial experience so customers make customisations, add data, and build something. The more they build in the product, the more they "own" it — and the higher the psychological cost of cancelling.

**Apply in freemium:** Make the free tier accumulate valuable data over time (history, settings, integrations). Cancelling means losing that accumulated value.

### Status-Quo Bias
People prefer the current state. Switching feels risky even when the alternative is objectively better.

**Apply:** Reduce the cost of switching. Migration tools, import features, "switch in 30 minutes" claims, and explicit before/after comparisons all address status-quo resistance. Never make the customer feel stupid for their current solution.

## Behavioural Design

### BJ Fogg Behaviour Model
**Behaviour = Motivation × Ability × Prompt**

All three must be present. Missing any one prevents the behaviour.

| Element | If missing | Fix |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| **Motivation** | Person doesn't care enough | Target the right person, or increase urgency/relevance |
| **Ability** | Person can't easily do it | Reduce friction — simplify the form, shorten the path, pre-fill fields |
| **Prompt** | Person doesn't think to do it | Add the CTA at the right moment — when motivation is highest |

**Apply in onboarding:** Map each step: is motivation present? Is the ability there (is the UX clear)? Is the prompt at the right moment?

### Hick's Law
Decision time increases logarithmically with the number of choices.

**Apply:** Every additional navigation item, pricing tier, or CTA competes with every other. The pricing page with 3 tiers converts better than 5 tiers. The email with 1 CTA gets more clicks than the email with 3.

### Default Effect
The default option is chosen significantly more often than other options, all else being equal.

**Apply:** Pre-select the plan you want customers on. Default the annual billing toggle. Default the recommended tier. Make the path you want them to take the path of least resistance.

### Nudge Theory
Small changes in presentation significantly influence decisions without restricting choice.

**Apply:** Which option is pre-checked? What happens when they leave a form field empty? What's the default state of the toggle? These "choice architecture" decisions have outsized conversion impact.

### Goal-Gradient Effect
People accelerate effort as they approach a goal. Partially-completed progress bars motivate more than empty ones.

**Apply:** Show progress in onboarding ("You're 60% set up"). Show progress in trials ("3 of 5 key features explored"). Start the progress bar at a non-zero value ("You've already done the hardest part — you signed up.")

### Zeigarnik Effect
Unfinished tasks occupy mental attention more than completed ones.

**Apply in onboarding emails:** "You're 80% done with your setup" creates tension that motivates return. The specific number matters — 80% feels completable; "almost done" doesn't.

**Apply in pricing:** "Your free trial expires in 3 days" works partly because of Zeigarnik — the open loop of an expiring trial creates mental occupation.

### Peak-End Rule
People judge an experience almost entirely by how it felt at its peak and at its end, not by the average.

**Apply in customer experience:** Design a wow moment early in the product (the "aha moment"). Ensure the end of the trial or last touchpoint before churn is positive, not frustrated. A great ending can recover a mediocre experience; a bad ending poisons a good one.

### IKEA Effect
People value things more when they've invested effort in creating them.

**Apply:** Don't over-simplify onboarding to the point of zero effort. Small, achievable setup tasks create ownership. A customer who configured their own dashboard cares more about it than one who was handed a pre-built template.

**Balance:** Too much effort = abandonment. The sweet spot is effort that creates ownership without creating friction.

## Copy Application Patterns

### The Curse of Knowledge
Once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it. Product teams write copy about features that means nothing to prospects who don't yet understand the context.

**Symptom:** Copy full of category jargon, product-specific terms, or "obvious" comparisons that aren't obvious to a newcomer.

**Fix:** Test every piece of copy with someone who doesn't work in the product. Ask them what it means. If they can't explain it back, rewrite it.

### Specificity Bias
Specific claims are more believable and more memorable than vague ones.

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|---------|
| "Saves you time" | "Saves 5 hours every Monday morning" |
| "Trusted by many companies" | "Trusted by 4,200 engineering teams" |
| "Grows your business" | "Customers see 40% more pipeline in 90 days" |
| "Easy to use" | "Set up in 20 minutes — no IT required" |

**The proof test:** Could a competitor use the same sentence? If yes, it's not specific enough.

### Contrast Effect
Comparisons dramatically change perception. The same item feels different depending on what it's shown next to.

**Apply in copy:** Show the before-state vividly before introducing the product. The contrast between "I used to spend my whole Sunday updating the board" and "now it takes 10 seconds" creates the emotional impact.

**Apply in pricing:** "Less than your daily coffee" and "less than one hour of your time" anchor the price against something familiar.

### Customer Language vs. Company Language
Customer language (their actual words, from reviews, calls, emails) almost always outconverts polished marketing copy.

**Detection rule:** If the sentence could appear in an investor deck or a press release, it's company language. If it sounds like something a person said in frustration or relief, it's customer language.

**Source hierarchy:** 1-star reviews → 5-star reviews → support tickets → sales call transcripts → forum posts → LinkedIn comments → polished interviews (least authentic).

## Pricing Psychology

### Charm Pricing
$99 feels significantly cheaper than $100. The left digit dominates psychological perception of price.

**Rule:** For value-positioning products, use charm pricing ($49, $99, $199). For premium positioning, use round numbers ($50, $100, $200) — round numbers signal quality, charm prices signal discounting.

### Price Relativity
Three tiers where the middle is the target: the expensive tier makes the middle seem reasonable; the cheap tier makes the middle seem high-quality.

The recommended tier should feel like the "sane" choice, not the "compromise" choice.

### Framing Time-Based Costs
"$1/day" activates a mental account of "trivial daily expenses" (coffee, parking). "$365/year" activates a "large annual commitment" account. Same money, different feeling.

Use the smallest time unit that stays credible. "$0.25/hour for a 40-hour workweek" is too abstract; "$89/month" activates the correct subscription-cost account.
